Section 1

Executive Summary

The San José Public Library Foundation is a well-run, audit-clean organization with one serious structural vulnerability: revenue concentration. Across FY2020–FY2024, contributions and grants supplied roughly 90%+ of revenue every year, and the City of San José alone ranged from 20% to 71% of total revenue — leaving the Foundation dependent on a single funder.

Three trends compound that exposure: private (non-City) giving has fallen about 65% since 2021 ($4.7M → $1.7M), the unrestricted operating cushion has stayed essentially flat near $1M while the organization grew, and the endowment remains negligible at ~2% of net assets.

The large FY2024 net-asset decline ($15.7M → $8.6M) is not a loss — it is the planned deployment of restricted City grants received in 2022. The strategic priority is therefore diversification and resilience: growing unrestricted reserves toward a 3–6 month policy, rebuilding private and multi-year revenue, and launching endowment growth — all consistent with the Foundation's stated value of Sustainability and its existing Epilogue Society planned-giving program.

Data limitation. This analysis relies solely on audited financial statements through FY2024 (year ended December 31, 2024). FY2025 audited results were not available at the time of writing, so the figures, ratios, and trends here reflect the position through 2024 and may not represent the Foundation's current financial situation. Any live discussion should confirm the most recent numbers.

Verified context (sjplf.org): mission — an educated, equitable, empowered, and engaged community; program framework — Educate, Engage, Empower; core value of Sustainability (financial continuity and fiscal responsibility); and an active planned-giving program, the Epilogue Society.

Section 3

Key Findings & Risks

Six findings from five years of audited data — each with the risk it creates, rated by severity and trend.

Finding 1 · Reassurance

The 2024 swing is planned, not distress

Net assets rose to $15.7M by end-2022 and fell to $8.6M by end-2024 — but that isn't a loss. In 2022 the Foundation booked ~$8.5M in City grants, most of it (~$8.2M) restricted for multi-year education programs. In 2024 it released $11.66M from restrictions and spent $13.04M, deploying that money into programs as intended. The $7.08M "decrease" is the spend-down of previously-received restricted funds, and net assets landed back near their 2021 level.

Risk: Board or funders could misread the planned 2024 spend-down as an operating loss — a reporting-optics issue, not a solvency one. Low · communication · one-time
Net assets by restriction
Restricted funds drive the swing
Finding 2 · Top risk

Revenue is dangerously concentrated in one funder

The City of San José swung from 20% → 30% → 71% → 41% → 61% of total revenue. With private giving, contributions and grants are ~90%+ of revenue every year, with almost no earned or diversified income. Critically, this City funding is discretionary — it flows through annual appropriations and program MOUs (such as SJ Learns, ~$1M/year since 2015), not a guaranteed obligation, and San José's voter-approved library parcel tax funds the Library department, not the Foundation. If a City contract is cut, delayed, or not renewed, both the restricted program revenue and the up-to-15% administrative fee that funds operations shrink together.

Risk: A cut, delay, or non-renewal of the City's discretionary, annually-appropriated grants would strike programs and the admin-fee revenue that funds operations at the same time. High · persistent

Finding 3 · Diversification

Private (non-City) giving is falling fast

Donations and miscellaneous grants dropped from $4.71M (2021) to $1.66M (2024) — down about 65% in three years. That is the diversification engine running in reverse: the very base that would cushion a City cut is shrinking.

Risk: The diversified base that would cushion a City cut is eroding year over year. High · worsening ↑

Finding 4 · Resilience

The unrestricted cushion is thin and flat

Unrestricted liquidity has hovered around $0.9M–$1.2M since 2021, and the board reserve (~$0.5M–$0.85M) has stayed essentially flat for five years while the organization grew. Against lumpy, largely-restricted cash flows, that buffer is thin — and in a nonprofit, staff can't be cut overnight if a grant slips.

Risk: Too little unrestricted buffer to absorb a grant delay or a sudden shock without disrupting programs or staff. Medium–High · flat →

Finding 5 · Opportunity

The endowment is negligible — and the biggest opportunity

The perpetual endowment is ~$210K, about 2% of net assets, so there is no permanent income cushion. Investment return — the only non-contribution revenue — is volatile, swinging to –$496K in 2022. Growing the endowment is the clearest lever to reduce reliance on annual funding cycles.

Risk: No permanent income stream, leaving the model exposed to annual funding cycles and investment-market swings. Medium · flat →

Also worth noting

Overhead is rising; the operator is disciplined

Management & general expense grew from $197K (2020) to $930K (2024) — about 4.7x — and fundraising cost per dollar raised rose from ~$0.05 to ~$0.29 as private giving shrank. On the positive side, this is a disciplined operator: clean audit opinions every year, ~80–89% of spending reaching programs, and Charity Navigator 4-star / Candid Platinum recognition.

Risk: Overhead growing faster than unrestricted revenue erodes financial flexibility over time. Medium · worsening ↑

Section 6

Revenue Innovation — From Strong Relationships to Durable Revenue

SJPLF is not starting from scratch. It already has an enviable base: major technology and foundation supporters including Apple, Adobe, Google, Samsung, Microsoft, Cisco and AMD, alongside Carnegie Corporation and the Silicon Valley Community Foundation; ~2,500 annual donors; a Signature Author Event; and the Epilogue Society. The challenge is not a lack of relationships — it is durability.

Despite that roster, revenue is still ~61% City, private giving has fallen ~65% since 2021, and the endowment sits at ~2% of net assets. So the opportunity is conversion, not acquisition: turning annual, transactional support into multi-year, compounding revenue. Each item below is an option to explore — SJPLF may already have work underway that isn't public.
